The Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS) Algorithm

The BFGS algorithm is one of the most known Quasi-Newton algorithms. This note briefly explores why the update of the approxiation of the inverse Hessian has the form that it has. I've done so because I've found that most texts lacks intuition and instead just states the update without any reasoning.

The Secant Condition

A key component of the derivation of the BFGS steps is the secant condition stating that

\[ H_{k+1} s_k = y_k \]

where \(s_k = x_{k+1} - x_k\) and \(y_k = \nabla f(x_{k+1}) - \nabla f(x_k)\).

Now define a quadratic approximation of \(f\) around \(x_{k+1}\) as

\[ \tilde{f}(x) = f(x_{k+1}) + \nabla f(x_{k+1})^T(x-x_{k+1}) + \frac{ 1 }{ 2 } (x - x_{k+1})^TH_{k+1}(x - x_{k+1}), \]

then

  • by construction we have that \(\nabla \tilde{f}(x_{k+1}) = \nabla f(x_{k+1})\)

  • while the secant condition ensures that also \(\nabla \tilde{f}(x_k) = \nabla f(x_k)\).

Rank-2 Updates

The main idea behind the BFGS algorithm is a simple (symmetric) rank-2 update of a proposed Hessian (note that because \(H_k\) is a proposed Hessian it must be symmetric)

\[\begin{aligned} H_{k + 1} &= H_k + \alpha uu^T + \beta vv^T\\ &= H_k + \begin{bmatrix} \alpha u & \beta v \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} u^T \\ v^T \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}\]

Given that \(H_{k+1}\) is simply \(H_k\) with a rank-\(2\) update we know from the Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury identity that we efficiently compute the inverse if the inverse of \(H_{k}\) is already known

\[\begin{aligned} \left( H_{k + 1} \right)^{-1} &= H_k^{-1} - H_{k}^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha u & \beta v \end{bmatrix} \left(I + \begin{bmatrix} u^T \\ v^T \end{bmatrix} H_{k}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \alpha u & \beta v \end{bmatrix} \right)^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} u^T \\ v^T \end{bmatrix}H_k^{-1} . \end{aligned}\]

In short this means that we only need to compute the full inverse on the first iteration while at any other point we can settle for just updating it through an inversion of a \(2\times 2\) matrix and a few matrix-vector products. While the idea is simlpe we still need to find suitable \(\alpha, \beta, u\) and \(v\) such that \(H_k\) is a positive definite matrix at all stages. Now set \(u=y_k\), \(v=H_ks_k\) and impose the secant condition (\(H_{k+1}s_k = y_k\)), then

\[\begin{aligned} H_{k+1}s_k &= H_ks_k + \underbrace{\alpha y_ky_k^Ts_k}_{\text{only term with $y_k$}} + \underbrace{\beta H_ks_ks_k^TH_ks_k}_{\text{only term with $H_k$}} = y_k. \end{aligned}\]

Now its clear that since the middle part has to be equal to \(y_k\) and only the \(\alpha\)-term contains \(y_k\) and only the \(\beta\)-term contains a \(H_ks_k\) we must have that

\[ \alpha = \frac{1}{y_k^Ts_k}, \quad \text{and} \quad \beta = -\frac{1}{s_k^TH_ks_k}. \]

Now this is give us an update strategy there is still something that is not particularily promising. Namely that in order to compute \(\beta\) we need to also store \(H_k\). However as shown in next section we can, through some tedious computations, show that we actually do not need to compute \(\beta\) at all.

Removing the depdence of \(H_k\) (Some tedious computations... )

Earlier we stated how the computation of the inverse could be reduced to a simple inversion of a \(2\times 2\) matrix an some matrix-vector products. However we later set \(v=H_ks_k\), meaning that we also needed to store \(H_k\) explicitly. Thankfully we can see that if we insert our chosen values of \(\alpha,\beta, u\) and \(v\) into the expression for the inversion we get something that does not explicitly depend on \(H_k\). The calculations are tedious and not nessecarily informing, however, given that these are notes I have chosen to include them anyways.

First inserting the expression we find that

\[\begin{aligned} \left( H_{k + 1} \right)^{-1} &= H_k^{-1} - \begin{bmatrix} \alpha H_{k}^{-1}y_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \left(I + \begin{bmatrix} y_k^T \\ s_k^TH_k \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha H_k^{-1}y_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \right)^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} y_k^TH_k^{-1} \\ s_k^T \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}\]

Now for the easy of notation introduce \(\gamma_k = H_k^{-1}y_k\)

\[\begin{aligned} \left( H_{k + 1} \right)^{-1} &= H_k^{-1} - \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \left(I + \begin{bmatrix} \alpha y_k^T\gamma_k & \beta y_k^Ts_k\\ \alpha s_k^Ty_k & \beta s_k^TH_ks_k\end{bmatrix} \right)^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} \gamma_k^T \\ s_k^T \end{bmatrix} \\ &= H_k^{-1} - \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \left(I + \begin{bmatrix} \alpha y_k^T\gamma_k & \beta \alpha^{-1}\\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix} \right)^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} \gamma_k^T \\ s_k^T \end{bmatrix}, \end{aligned}\]

where we used that \(\beta s_k^TH_ks_k = -1\). One could be be tempted to stop here, as the expression does not look to depend on \(H_k\). However one needs to remember that \(\beta\) is defined using \(H_k\). We must therefore continue

\[\begin{aligned} \left( H_{k + 1} \right)^{-1} &= H_k^{-1} - \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 + \alpha y_k^T\gamma_k & \beta \alpha^{-1}\\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix} \right)^{-1}\begin{bmatrix} \gamma_k^T \\ s_k^T \end{bmatrix} \\ &= H_k^{-1} - \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \frac{-\alpha}{\beta}\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\beta \alpha^{-1}\\ -1 & 1 + \alpha y_k^T\gamma_k\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \gamma_k^T \\ s_k^T \end{bmatrix} \\ &= H_k^{-1} + \frac{\alpha}{\beta}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -\beta \alpha^{-1}s_k^T\\ -\gamma_k^T + s_k^T + \alpha y_k^T\gamma_ks_k^T\end{bmatrix}\\ &= H_k^{-1} + \frac{\alpha}{\beta}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \gamma_k & \beta s_k \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -\beta \alpha^{-1}s_k^T\\ -\gamma_k^T + s_k^T + \alpha y_k^T\gamma_ks_k^T\end{bmatrix}\\ &=H_k^{-1} + \frac{\alpha}{\beta}\left( -\beta \gamma_ks_k^T - \beta s_k\gamma_k^T + \beta s_ks_k^T + \beta\alpha s_ky_k^T\gamma_ks_k^T \right)\\ &= H_k^{-1} + \left( -\alpha \gamma_ks_k^T - \alpha s_k\gamma_k^T + \alpha s_ks_k^T + \alpha^2 s_ky_k^T\gamma_ks_k^T \right)\\ &= H_k^{-1} + (\alpha + \alpha^2y_k^T\gamma_k)s_ks_k^T - \alpha(\gamma_ks_k^T + s_k\gamma_k^T). \end{aligned}\]

As promised we see that the update of the inverse does not explictly rely on \(H_k\). The expression is usually stated with \(\gamma_k = H_k^{-1}y_k\) reinserted. However that is misleading given that a good implementation would have rather compute the temporary variable \(\gamma_k\). Now that from the last expression it is also clear that the update will continue to be symmetric, as \((\alpha \alpha^2y_k^T\gamma_k)\) is just a scalar and \((\gamma_ks_k^T + s_k\gamma_k^T)\) is clearly symmetric.
