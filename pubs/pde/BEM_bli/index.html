Boundary Layer Impedance

It has been suggested by Berggren et al. [1], that viscothermal effects can be approximated by asserting a Wentzell boundary condition on the Helmholtz equation. In short the takeaway from the paper is that the viscothermal effects can be approximated by solving

\[\begin{aligned} \Delta p + k^2p &= 0, \qquad y \in \Omega\\ \delta\frac{i-1}{2}\Delta_tp - \delta_Tk^2\frac{(i-1)(\gamma-1))}{2}p &= \frac{\partial p}{\partial n}, \quad y \in \partial\Omega. \end{aligned}\]

An equivalent formulation of the boundary condition is in terms of an admittance [2]

\[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial n} = ikY_{\text{BLI}}p, \quad \text{where} \quad Y_{\text{BLI}} = \left(\frac{1}{k_h} - \frac{\Delta_t}{k_vk} \right). \]

Simply inserting this into the representation formula we get the following

\[\begin{aligned} C(P)p(P) &= \int_\Gamma \frac{\partial G(R)}{\partial n}p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma - \int_\Gamma G(R)\frac{\partial p(Q)}{\partial n}\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma\\ &= \int_\Gamma \frac{\partial G(R)}{\partial n}p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma - ik\int_\Gamma G(R)\left(\frac{1}{k_h} - \frac{\Delta_t}{k_vk} \right)p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma. \end{aligned}\]

Now applying integration by parts on the tangential Laplacian, \(\Delta_tp\), it follows that

\[ C(P)p(P) = \int_\Gamma \frac{\partial G(R)}{\partial n}p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma - \frac{ik}{k_h}\int_\Gamma G(R)p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma + \frac{i}{k_v}\int_\Gamma \nabla_t G(R)\nabla_t p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma \]

where it was assumed that

\[ \int_{\Lambda} G(R)\nabla_tp(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Lambda = 0, \]

and \(\Lambda\) represents the boundary of \(\Gamma\). Given that all of the intergrals are over the full boundary we can rewrite the representation formulas as follows

\[\begin{aligned} C(P)p(P) = \int_\Gamma \left(\frac{\partial G(R)}{\partial n} - \frac{ik}{k_h}G(R) + \frac{i}{k_v} \nabla_t G(R)\nabla_t\right)p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma. \end{aligned}\]

Defining the expression inside of the parentheses as \(K(R)\) we can further simplify things to

\[ C(P)p(P) = \int_\Gamma K(R)p(Q)\ \mathrm{d}\Gamma. \]

While the above seems easily implemented there is one part of \(K(R)\) which is not easily computed, namely that of numerically intestimating the tangential derivative \(\nabla_t\). In 2D this could be done by a simple finite difference, however things are different in 3D, specially if the interpolation grids are unstructured. A method of computing this derivative is explained in the note on Shape Function Derivatives, however, if one would naively implement this one would see that this requires a inversion of a 3x3-matrix for every gaussian interpolation node. As such this would be a very time consuming computation. Do we have a solution for this yet?
